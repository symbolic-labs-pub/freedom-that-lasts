### From Choice Maximization to Option-Space Preservation

**Miklós Béky**

## **Abstract**

Modern societies overwhelmingly define freedom as the maximization of present choice under formal rights. Yet empirical observation shows that even well-intentioned democratic systems recurrently drift into rigidity, debt accumulation, institutional paralysis, and crisis-driven centralization. This paper proposes a reframing of freedom as a *system-level stability property*: the preservation of future option space under uncertainty and disagreement. Drawing on game theory, control theory, institutional economics, and systems design, we demonstrate why classical equilibria (Pareto efficiency and Nash stability) are insufficient to protect long-term freedom in repeated collective decision environments. We then outline an institutional architecture—based on bounded reversibility, mandatory feedback, constrained step-size adjustment, capability-based execution, and revocable delegation—that admits *option-stable equilibria* without requiring altruism, consensus, or exceptional leadership. The framework is human-governed, technology-agnostic, and compatible with democratic legitimacy. Freedom, we argue, is not an achieved state but a continuously maintained condition.

---

## **1. Introduction**

Political theory has long treated freedom as a normative property of individuals: the absence of coercion, the availability of choice, or the possession of rights. While these formulations remain ethically foundational, they are increasingly insufficient to explain real-world outcomes in complex societies.

Across diverse institutional contexts, democratic systems repeatedly exhibit the same pathologies:

* accumulation of irreversible commitments,
* delayed or absent correction,
* rising dependence on crisis governance,
* and shrinking room for future maneuver.

These outcomes often occur **without authoritarian intent**, raising a fundamental question:

> How can systems built to protect freedom reliably erode it over time?

This paper argues that the failure lies not primarily in values or actors, but in **institutional dynamics**. Freedom must be analyzed not only as a moral concept, but as a *stability property of repeated collective decision systems*.

---

## **2. Conceptual Framework: Freedom as Future Option Space**

We define **freedom** operationally as:

> The size and connectivity of the future option space available to a society.

Let $\Omega_{t}$ denote the set of viable system states reachable from time $t$. Freedom is not the immediate selection among options at $t$, but the preservation of $|\Omega_{t+k}|$ for future $k$.

This reframing yields three important implications:

1. Freedom is **temporal**, not static.
2. Freedom is **systemic**, not reducible to individual acts.
3. Freedom can be **silently consumed** through accumulation of irreversible decisions.

---

## **3. Why Classical Stability Concepts Fail**

### 3.1 Pareto Efficiency

Pareto-dominant decisions improve at least one agent’s payoff without harming others in the present. However, Pareto efficiency is **myopic**: it does not account for option-space erosion across time.

A decision can be Pareto-improving today while destroying future reversibility.

### 3.2 Nash Equilibrium

Nash-stable outcomes arise when no agent can improve their payoff by unilateral deviation. In repeated governance games with delayed feedback and shared future costs, Nash equilibria frequently incentivize overcommitment and under-correction.

Thus:

> Nash stability does not imply freedom preservation.

---

## **4. Option-Stable Equilibria**

We introduce the concept of an **option-stable equilibrium**, defined as a state where:

1. No agent benefits from unilateral deviation,
2. No coalition benefits from deviation over time,
3. The expected future option space is non-decreasing:

$$
\mathbb{E}[|\Omega_{t+k}|] \ge |\Omega_{t}|
$$

Such equilibria do not emerge spontaneously under unconstrained optimization. They require **institutional structure**.

---

## **5. Institutional Design Principles**

We outline five structural mechanisms that jointly alter payoff landscapes to admit option-stable equilibria.

### 5.1 Bounded Reversibility

All decisions are classified by reversibility class, constraining:

* approval thresholds,
* scaling speed,
* and correction latency.

Irreversibility is treated as a scarce resource.

---

### 5.2 Mandatory Feedback and Review

All decisions enter a lifecycle with:

* scheduled evaluation,
* automatic review triggers,
* and predefined correction pathways.

Persistence without review is structurally prohibited.

---

### 5.3 Budget Step-Size Constraints

Budget adjustments are bounded by adaptive step sizes:

$$
|\Delta B| \le \alpha \cdot B
$$

where $\alpha$ depends on reversibility, capacity, and performance.

Citizens vote on *direction*, not magnitude.

---

### 5.4 Capability-Based Execution

Procurement is eligibility-based rather than ranked:

* binary requirements,
* evidence-backed capability claims,
* non-discretionary selection among eligible suppliers.

Execution is measured by observable events, not reports.

---

### 5.5 Revocable Delegation

Decision authority is:

* granular,
* revocable in real time,
* and hierarchically composable.

Power accumulates conditionally, not permanently.

---

## **6. Failure Modes and Graceful Degradation**

The system is explicitly designed to fail *locally* rather than *catastrophically*.

Under stress:

* ambition contracts,
* reversibility dominates,
* irreversible actions halt.

No failure pathway silently locks in future loss of freedom.

---

## **7. The Role of Technology and AI**

The framework does not require artificial intelligence to function. However, AI may be used to:

* reduce cognitive load via summaries,
* translate legal language into plain explanations,
* compare historical decisions,
* and surface early warning signals.

AI is strictly **non-authoritative**. All binding decisions remain human-controlled.

---

## **8. Discussion**

This framework challenges three deeply embedded assumptions:

1. That freedom is maximized by speed,
2. That legitimacy requires finality,
3. That stability requires consensus.

Instead, it proposes that:

* restraint enables continuity,
* corrigibility sustains legitimacy,
* and disagreement can coexist with durability.

---

## **9. Conclusion**

Freedom does not disappear primarily through oppression, but through **unnoticed accumulation of irreversible decisions**.

Reframing freedom as a stability property of decision systems reveals why many democracies drift toward rigidity despite good intentions—and how institutional design can counteract that drift without ideology, technological dependence, or moral heroics.

Freedom, properly understood, is not declared once.
It is maintained continuously.
